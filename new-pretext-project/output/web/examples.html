    Section 3 Preliminary Examples

    Let \(k=3\) and \(d = 6\text{.}\) The proofs later will show that taking \(N = (d+1) \cdot 2^{k-1} = 7 \cdot 2^{2} =28\) and \(n\) an odd integer greater than \(N = 28\text{,}\) say \(n = 29\text{,}\) then all the digits of \(29^3\) in base-\(58\) are greater than \(6\text{.}\) This really means expressing \(29^3 = 24,389 = 7 \cdot 58^2 + 14 \cdot 58 + 29\text{.}\) The ‚Äúdigits‚Äù are greater than \(d = 6\text{.}\) Note that the final ‚Äúdigit‚Äù of \(29^3\) in base-\(58\) is \(n=29\) itself. This will be proved to be true in general in the later sections.

    On the other hand, consider the even number \(30\) greater than \(N = 28\text{.}\) Then \(30^3 = 27,000\) epressed in base-\(60\) is \(7 \cdot 60^{2} + 30 \cdot 60 + 0\) and the last ‚Äúdigit‚Äù \(0\) fails to be greater than \(6\text{.}\)
    üîó
    For some intuition why \(N = (d+1) c\dot 2^{k-1}\) suffices, consider that \(n^k \lt (2n)^k\text{,}\) so sufficiently large \(n\) the base-\(2n\) expansion will have at most \(k\) digits from \(0\) to \(k-1\text{.}\) For sufficiently large \(n\text{,}\) \(n^k\) will have exactly \(k\) digits, with no leading zeros. To at least meet the requirement, the expansion needs \(n^k \ge (d+1)(2n)^{k-1}\text{.}\) Canceling \(n^{k-1}\) gives \(n \ge (d+1)2^{k-1}\text{.}\) The proofs will show it is also sufficient for the remaining digits.
    üîó
    As some further examples, consider the following table of odd numbers \(n\) raised to some low powers \(k\) and then expressed in base-\(2n\text{.}\) The expressions have their ‚Äúdigits‚Äù expressed as decimal numbers, with a ‚Äúdigit‚Äù in parentheses if the base is greater than \(10\text{.}\) Note that as expected the final digit is always \(n\text{.}\) Furthermore the digits are increasing, so it might be suspected that given \(k\) and \(d\) there is some \(N\) such that for \(n > N\text{,}\) the digits of \(n^k\) are greater than \(d\text{.}\)
